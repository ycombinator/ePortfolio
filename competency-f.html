Introduction

Collection development and collection management are vital skills for any librarian. The two principle factors affecting print collections are the physical space they require and the audience for whom they are intended. Creation of new collections requires dedicated storage space, and selection of materials requires knowledge of the patron base. Evaluation of a print collection involves usage statistics for each item and the weeding out of unused items that are taking up precious shelf space. Acquisition of materials into the permanent collection requires budget latitude, but collections can also be temporarily bolstered through loan programs. Protecting and preserving a print collection calls for efforts to locate missing items, repairing broken or damaged bindings, and requesting replacement parts when necessary. The organization of a print collection is typically based on the physical space and is done in an effort to collocate similar subjects. Digital collections are constrained less by physical space and more by disk space. Additionally, cost is a major factor in the development of digital collections. Subscriptions to electronic journals and large e-journal databases are often prohibitively expensive for libraries. They also run the risk of losing the archived content if the database is discontinued or the library must cancel its contract.

Evidence

The bulk of my evidence in support of this competency comes from the Final Report I wrote for LIBR 294: Professional Experience - Internships. Two of my learning outcomes were directly related to collection management, and I was able to gain quite a bit of hands-on experience with every aspect of Competency F. To begin, I was able to practice creating a new collection by selecting a set of recommended resources for new associates. As seen in Appendix B of the report, I culled together a list of vetted resources, both electronic and print, on a variety of topics that would be most useful to new attorneys. The topics were chosen based on the practice areas of the law firm, and the sources were selected based on the advice of librarians and field experts. The beauty of this new collection is that it is formed from materials already found in the firm's library; they have simply been regrouped in such a way to make them more useful and easier to find when a topical question arises.

As the firm's shelf space was incredibly limited, Joanne Scala (the firm's librarian) faced a constant need to evaluate the print collection. She tracked usage loosely by asking that attorneys ask her to fetch books for them. Items that were infrequently used were noted down and permission was sought from the corporate headquarters to dispose of them. Joanne told me that she suspected that several volumes of two different titles were going unused. In the first case (see p. 6), we observed that the older edits to securities laws were never touched (and had gathered quite a bit of dust). On Joanne's urging, I pulled several of the Federal Securities Law Reporter transfer binders, dating back nearly a decade, off the shelf. Once the head librarian had granted permission, Joanne asked me to dismantle the binders and recycle the contents. The second case was trickier. The firm maintained a print subscription to the California state code. These books, numbering in the hundreds, took up eight shelves. Joanne had been monitoring usage, and found that while some of the volumes did get used, the frequency was low. Although no resolution was made before the end of my internship, I was able to confirm with Joanne after the fact, that her reasoning was sound. With the approval of the firm's head librarian, she canceled the print subscription in favor of an electronic one (through Lexis) and donated the volumes to a local library. I learned that evaluating a collection for the purpose of weeding is a weighty responsibility. There is bound to be a patron who is dissatisfied with a weeding decision. Hopefully, as I will discuss next, the librarian will be able to help the patron acquire the material he seeks through other means.

Acquisitioning is the next aspect of collection management that I learned about through my internship. One of the easiest ways to satisfy a short-term need for a material is through an inter-library loan (ILL). Many local and academic libraries belong to consortiums that have an agreement to share materials. These networks are invaluable to the patron needing a book not available at her local branch library. Likewise, the firm where I interned was networked in several ways to give and receive ILLs. Joanne used her memberships in several local law librarian associations to seek ILLs, and she also secured borrowing privileges from the Stanford University Law Library. Pages 15 to 17 and Appendix C of my internship report explain in detail how I used the email listserv of one local association to foster goodwill by offering a book to a local firm and also to seek out two volumes that our firm needed on a short-term basis. Longer-term needs typically required a permanent addition to the firm's collection. I assisted Joanne by acquiring one law review article from an obscure publisher in Pennsylvania (p. 18), and attempted to locate and secure another article from a foreign publisher, but was unsuccessful because the article did not exist (p. 17).

Legal print materials are expensive; preserving them is not only prudent, but also necessary for law libraries. In my capacity as intern, I assisted Joanne in determining which volumes had gone missing from the collection. Using my shelf list (Appendix A), Joanne then went office by office to determine where the missing books might be. Those that were deemed lost would either need to be replaced or removed from the catalog. Locating and reshelving these missing materials served as a method of preserving the integrity of the physical collection. I also helped Joanne repair binders and contact vendors to replace any binders that were beyond repair or loose-leaf pages that were found to be missing (p. 6).

Lastly, I spent several weeks assisting Joanne in her efforts to organize her collection. Due to the dispersed nature of the shelving, a conventional classification system was not practical. Joanne and I discussed the options and determined that a color-coded spine label system was a better choice. Joanne ordered the labeling supplies, and I created the colored labels using abbreviations to indicate the shelf location ("1W" = first floor, west wing). Next, we chose the topical arrangement of the shelves. For example, one section held corporate practice materials on securities, mergers, statutes, accounting rules and patent information. I then affixed the labels to the spines of each volume in the given section (pp. 6-7). Subject classification or organization is most common in libraries, and it allows for serendipitous finds through browsing. In a series of discussion posts for LIBR 210: Reference and Information Services, I weigh the costs and benefits of moving reference materials out of a reference collection and into the circulating collection. In this particular case, those needing to browse by subject would gain by having the topical reference materials on the same shelves as other related books, but those looking for vetted, reliable answers would no longer have the confidence that a known reference material would provide.

Conclusion

Through my extensive hands-on internship experience and reflective discussion posts, I have shown that I can use the basic concepts and principles related to the creation, evaluation, selection, acquisition, preservation and organization of information collections. I really enjoyed helping to manage the law firm's print collection and look forward to doing similar work as a full-time librarian.
